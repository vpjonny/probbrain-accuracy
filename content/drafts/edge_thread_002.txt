TWEET 1 (hook):
---
Before you look at a single piece of evidence about a specific event, there's one question you should always ask first:

"What is the base rate for this category of event?"

Most forecasters skip it entirely. It's also the highest-leverage step in the whole process.

[1/7]

---

TWEET 2 (what is reference class forecasting):
---
The technique is called reference class forecasting. It was formalized by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky. Bent Flyvbjerg later showed it predicts megaproject outcomes better than expert judgment.

The idea:
1. Identify a class of comparable events
2. Find the historical frequency (the base rate)
3. Use that as your starting probability
4. Then — and only then — adjust for case-specific details

[2/7]

---

TWEET 3 (the inside vs. outside view):
---
Kahneman called this the "outside view" vs. the "inside view."

Inside view: You analyze the specific situation in depth. You build a narrative. You reach a conclusion.

Outside view: You step back and ask what usually happens to situations like this one.

The inside view feels more rigorous. It's usually less accurate.

[3/7]

---

TWEET 4 (the hard part — choosing the reference class):
---
The hardest part of reference class forecasting isn't the math. It's choosing the right reference class.

Too narrow: "Elections in this exact country under these exact conditions" → not enough data points.
Too wide: "All political events ever" → base rate is meaningless.

The skill is finding a class broad enough to have statistical signal, narrow enough to be relevant.

[4/7]

---

TWEET 5 (worked example):
---
Concrete example: A peace negotiation is announced. Market prices "ceasefire signed within 6 months" at 60%.

Reference class: Formal ceasefire negotiations in active territorial conflicts since 1990.
Historical frequency: ~35% result in a signed agreement within 6 months.

That's a 25-point gap before we've looked at a single specific detail about this conflict.

[5/7]

TWEET 6 (how we apply it):
---
Our process at ProbBrain:

1. Assign event to a category (conflict, election, central bank decision, etc.)
2. Pull the base rate from historical data
3. That number becomes our prior
4. We then update using current evidence — polling, momentum, precedent
5. Compare final estimate to market price

Step 1–3 takes 10 minutes. It prevents the most common forecasting error.
