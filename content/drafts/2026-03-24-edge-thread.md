## TWEET 2 (the bias)

Most forecasters commit base rate neglect.

They anchor on the specific event in front of them and ignore the category it belongs to.

"Will this ceasefire hold?" isn't a novel question. It's one data point in a long series of ceasefire agreements — most of which collapse within 18 months.

The base rate exists. Most people skip it.

[2/5]

---

## TWEET 3 (markets aren't immune)

Prediction markets are better than pundits. But they're not immune.

Recency bias and base rate neglect are correlated: when striking news drops, crowds overweight it and reprice hard — often too hard.

There's a predictable window after major news breaks where markets drift away from what the historical record actually implies.

That's the gap we look for.

[3/5]

---

## TWEET 4 (our process)

Our framework, plainly stated:

1. Start with the base rate for the event category
2. Let post-news markets settle (~48–72 hrs)
3. Generate our probability estimate: base rate + current evidence
4. Compare to market price
5. If the gap exceeds our threshold, we call it

The edge isn't being smarter. It's being less reactive than a crowd responding to yesterday's headlines.
