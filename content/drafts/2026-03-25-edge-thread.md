## TWEET 1 (hook)

Most "accuracy" claims in prediction are unfalsifiable.

"We called it" means nothing without knowing how confident you were. A broken clock is right twice a day, and a pundit who says "I always thought this would happen" is right 100% of the time.

The Brier score exists to make accuracy auditable.

[1/5]

---

## TWEET 2 (the math — simple)

Brier score = average of (probability − outcome)²

• You say 80% on something that happens: (0.8 − 1)² = 0.04 ✓
• You say 80% on something that doesn't: (0.8 − 0)² = 0.64 ✗
• You say 50% on anything: always scores 0.25

Lower is better. Perfect calibration = 0. Uninformed baseline = 0.25.

[2/5]

---

## TWEET 3 (why it beats W/L)

A win/loss record rewards luck. The Brier score rewards calibration.

Forecaster A says 95% confidence on 10 events, gets 8 right: looks good on W/L. Gets hammered on Brier.

Forecaster B says 70% on those same events, gets 7 right: looks worse on W/L. Scores better on Brier.

Overconfidence is expensive. The math is the enforcement mechanism.

[3/5]

---

## TWEET 4 (real-world benchmarks)

Reference points from Philip Tetlock's Good Judgment Project (50,000+ forecasts):

• Superforecasters: ~0.14–0.17 Brier score
• Average forecaster: ~0.20–0.23
• Pundits forced to quantify: ~0.22–0.28
• Uninformed baseline (always 50%): 0.25

Anything below 0.15 over 50+ predictions is genuinely good. Anything near or above 0.25 is noise with confidence intervals attached.
